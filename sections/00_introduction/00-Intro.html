Introduction

The concept of FAIR Digital Objects (FDOs) emerged from discussions in a) the Research Data Alliance where Working and Interest Groups discussed – drawing upon a seminal paper by Kahn and Wilensky [[Kahn-Wilensky]] – worked on all aspects around Digital Objects as replacement for the file-oriented approach which is still being used and creating so many inefficiencies and b) the FAIR community where machine actionability is a key issue [[Jacobsen2020]].

A FAIR Digital Object (FDO) is a harmonized and persistent bundle of information that represents (i.e., identifies, encapsulates, and describes) a (digital or physical) resource which can be of any type (data, metadata, semantic assertions, configurations, AI models, etc.). FDOs implement the FAIR principles [[FAIR]] and comply by-default with these principles:

  • Each resource (which is represented as an FDO) is identified by a globally unique, resolvable, and persistent identifier (a PID).
  • A PID resolves into a structured, machine-actionable metadata record that describes the identified resource.
  • The record contains external reference(s) to the data bit-sequence of the resource and additional metadata bit-sequence(s), if appropriate.
  • An FDO contains at least license, provenance, and domain-specific metadata that follow agreed upon standards.

FDOs can be seen not only as an implementation of the FAIR principles but also as a way to improve data sovereignty, transparency/traceability, accountability, and persistence. These characteristics are gaining even more importance when establishing a stable, robust, and highly distributed ecosystem of data, which includes well-documented AI models and their training corpus. Societies and business will increasingly depend on proper AI models, requesting a reliable and trustworthy data basis. FDOs are passive units of information that posess the ability to be associated with executable operations. This does not only enable encapsulation but can also be used to support autonomously acting agents. Due to these properties, FDOs are a common harmonization layer for the many dataspaces that have been developed and which currently are emerging in science and industry. FDO inform the three layers of dataspaces: creating trust amongst partners, comparing rights on resources with user claims, and finally exchanging the data.
