The concept of FAIR Digital Objects (FDOs) emerged from discussions in a) the Research Data Alliance where Working and Interest Groups discussed - drawing upon a seminal paper by Kahn and Wilensky (Kahn & Wilensky 2006) - worked on all aspects around Digital Objects as replacement for the file oriented approach which is still being used and creating so many inefficiencies and b) the FAIR community where machine actionability is a key issue (Jacobsen et al. 2020).

A FAIR Digital Object (FDO) is a standardised and persistent bundle of information that represents (i.e., identifies, encapsulates and describes) a (digital or physical) resource which can be of any type (data, metadata, semantic assertions, configurations, AI models, etc.). FDOs implement the FAIR principles. As such, they comply to the following FAIR guidelines:

  • Each resource (which is represented as an FDO) is identified by a globally unique, resolvable, and persistent identifier (PID).
  • The PID resolves into a structured, machine actionable metadata record that describes the identified resource.
  • The record contains external reference(s) to the data bit-sequence of the resource and additional metadata bit-sequence(s), if appropriate.
  • The FDO contains at least license, provenance, and domain-specific metadata that follow agreed upon standards.

FDOs can be seen not only as an implementation of the FAIR principles but also as a way to improve data sovereignty, transparency/traceability, accountability and persistence. These characteristics will be increasingly important when establishing a stable, robust and highly distributed ecosystem of data, which includes well-documented AI models and their training corpus. Societies and business will be increasingly dependent on proper AI models, requesting a reliable and trustworthy data basis. FDOs are passive units of information, however increasingly often they will be associated with operations which not only enable encapsulation but can also be used to implement autonomously acting agents. Due to their properties, FDOs are also very well positioned to be used as common basic building blocks for gateways to the many dataspaces that have been developed and which currently are emerging in science and industry. The FDO bundle can inform the 3 layers of dataspaces: creating trust amongst partners, comparing rights on resources with user claims, and finally exchanging the data.
